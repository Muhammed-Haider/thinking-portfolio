## Case Studies

### AI Hiring Tool Bias Dilemma

**Dilemma**: An AI hiring tool reduces time-to-hire by 60% but shows statistically significant bias against candidates from certain universities.

**Arguments Against Using the Tool**:
1. **Lack of Merit**: Eliminates merit-based hiring, undermining organizational integrity.
2. **Promotes Bigotry**: Reinforces discriminatory practices within the organization.
3. **Ethical Concerns**: Unethical behavior affects the organization, targeted university communities, societal culture, and fosters resentment among affected groups.

**Counterargument That Could Change Perspective**:
- **Time Savings**: Significantly reduces hiring time, which is critical in competitive markets where speed matters most.

## Reflection on This Position

### My Position
- An AI hiring tool cuts time-to-hire by 60% but is biased against candidates from certain universities.
- Arguments: 1) It means no merit in the organization, 2) Creates bigotry in the organization, 3) It is not ethical.
- Stakeholders identified: organization, people at the affected universities, society and culture, resentment among people from those universities.
- Main counter: saving time while hiring matters the most.

### Evaluation Summary
- Argument strength ratings:
  1. No merit in the organization — 6/10.
  2. Creates bigotry — 7/10.
  3. Not ethical — 5/10.
- Stakeholder cost matrix: good start, but it misses several affected groups and could name who bears the cost more precisely.
- Confidence level: slightly overconfident; the claims are strong but need more evidence and clearer definitions.
- Strongest counter-argument: the efficiency gain may be seen as more valuable than fairness if hiring speed is a strategic priority, especially when the bias is not immediately visible.
- Missed stakeholders: rejected applicants outside the targeted universities, hiring managers/recruiters, legal and compliance teams, customers, investors, and broader underrepresented communities.

### Preliminary Grades
- Position Clarity: 7/10
- Argument Strength: 5/10
- Stakeholder Awareness: 6/10
- Intellectual Honesty: 7/10

### Thinking Score Card
- Independent Thinking: 7/10 — The position is self-generated and not merely copied from an external source.
- Critical Evaluation: 6/10 — Some important flaws are recognized, but the reasoning still lacks depth and evidence.
- Reasoning Depth: 5/10 — The argument is surface-level and would benefit from more concrete examples and definitions.
- Originality: 6/10 — The concern about biased AI hiring is relevant, though the framing is fairly standard.
- Self-Awareness: 6/10 — There is some awareness of the main counterargument, but not enough engagement with weaker claims.

### Attack on the Position
1. The claim “no merit in the organization” is too broad and vague; it assumes the tool completely replaces merit-based evaluation rather than potentially speeding parts of the process while still allowing merit-based review.
2. Saying the tool “creates bigotry” is emotionally charged but unproven; it confuses a biased output with institutional prejudice without showing how hiring decisions are deliberately discriminatory.
3. The ethical claim is underdefined; without a clear standard of fairness or harm, “not ethical” is just an opinion and does not address whether the bias is correctable or outweighed by competitive necessity.

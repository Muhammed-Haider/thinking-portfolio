This is my decision trail 

Stage 1 — Original decision
Your instinct was sound. "Action produces information" is a real strategic principle, not a rationalization. Launching to learn is a defensible position under genuine uncertainty.
The problems were in the scaffolding around it. 80% confidence with no ARR data, no customer signal, and no competitive pricing information is a number without a foundation. You were 80% confident in a principle, not in this specific decision — those are different things and you didn't separate them. Your reversal triggers were "customer feedback" three times. That is a category, not a trigger. A trigger requires a metric, a threshold, and a timeframe. Without those three components, you cannot actually pull it when the moment arrives.

Stage 2 — Post-consultation
The structure improved. You introduced the right concepts: controlled beta, real user data, competitor validation, defined timeframe. That is a better decision on paper.
But you replicated the exact failure mode your own process document names: structural completion without operational depth. "Controlled beta" — how many users? "High-quality signals" — which metrics? "Defined timeframe" — how many days? Every phrase that sounds rigorous is doing invisible work that never gets done. Confidence was also never restated numerically. You had a more structured position but an equally unanchored confidence level.

Stage 3 — Post-information drop
This is the most important stage and the one with the biggest gap. The new information was the most significant input in the entire trail: 34% of ARR in active competitor evaluation, and the cited concern was not product gaps but confidence in your roadmap stability. That changes the nature of the problem entirely. It is not a product problem. It is a trust problem with three specific, identifiable accounts.
You correctly did not abandon your position. That took genuine conviction and it was the right call. You correctly identified that a rushed visible launch would read as panic. You added a PR narrative to address that. Those are good instincts.
What you missed: the information told you the channel matters as much as the action. A press release is a broadcast. The three accounts you are worried about will read it alongside everyone else, on the market's timeline, not yours. What those accounts needed was a direct phone call before any public signal — reaching them first, privately, with a clear articulation of your roadmap direction, so that when the launch became visible they had already heard from you personally. Same action. Different sequencing. Substantially different effect on the relationship.
The other gap at this stage: you stated no confidence level at all. This is the one stage where a written confidence statement was most important, because the stakes just went up materially. Omitting it means you acted on a feeling rather than an analyzed position.

Adaptation quality
Proportional, not overreaction or underreaction. You held your position when the evidence justified holding it. The underreaction is narrower — you reached for the right intervention concept (manage the panic perception) but the wrong tool (broadcast PR instead of direct relationship call).

Anchoring vs. evidence-based revision
You revised based on evidence, which is correct. Anchoring would be refusing to modify anything after committing. You absorbed new information, identified the specific threat, and added a mitigation layer. That is not anchoring. The issue is that your core assumption — that these accounts would receive a launch as a confidence signal — was directly challenged by the new information, and your revision addressed the surface of that challenge without reaching the root.

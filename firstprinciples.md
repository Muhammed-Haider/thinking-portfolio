## Startups and MVPs

Startups should build an MVP before investing in scale.

Yes, this is true generally and is the best practice in software, but there are multiple startups for which building an MVP would not be sufficient to test them. For example, LinkedIn has no utility if the user base is 10, 100, or even 1,000. The platform shows its value only when significant scale is achieved. If there are users and no recruiters, or 100 recruiters and no users, it seems like a useless social networking site.

Other examples include Amazon. It shows its power at scale. When it has hundreds and thousands of buyers and sellers, it starts making money for both sides and shows its true value. If it had stayed small, what possible value could it have delivered? That is why Amazon became profitable by the 17th year of its founding.

Uber with one car seems like a taxi service or a simple cab service. But with scale and many cars, as the number of users increased, the number of cars increased, the time for the service to reach the customer decreased, the quality improved, and cars became cheap while more cars were summoned so drivers were making more money.
